Investigating the neural signal for error processing

Background

The error-related negativity (ERN) is a neural signal that is generated in the brain when people make mistakes. It is thought to be the brain's performance monitoring mechanism used to detect and correct potential errors. While this ERN responses have been detected using various cognitive to motor tasks, the research has largely been restricted to non-invasive recordings, limiting our understanding of the neural mechanisms of the ERN. Moreover, vast majority of electrophysiological recordings examined the ERN resulting from single trial-based and discretely time-locked responses using highly artificial tasks. However, with growing importance in understanding the brainâ€™s error-related system in the context of brain-computer interface (BCI), studying the brain's error processing in the context of naturalistic task is critical.

Current Study

In this study, we sought to study the ERNR using intracranial encephalography (iEEG) while subjects carried out a continuous performance monitoring task (Flappy Bird). Through our study, we demonstrate that the ERNR can be observed during continuous performance monitoring of errors in a more natural context, in many different brain areas including presupplementary motor area, orbitofrontal cortex, insula, and anterior cingulate cortex, replicating the results of earlier intracranial, EEG, and fMRI studies. Additionally, we demonstrate that brain regions sensitive to errors still show error-related neural responses even when no error has occurred, likely driven by high likelihood of error.
